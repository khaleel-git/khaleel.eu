The Theorem & Variables

Bayes' Formula

$$P(D|T) = \frac{P(D) \cdot P(T|D)}{P(T)}$$
\(P(D)\) — Prior (Prevalence)

The baseline probability of having the disease before any test is done.

\(P(T|D)\) — Sensitivity (Likelihood)

The probability of a positive test given the patient has the disease.

Formula Cheat Sheet

Handwritten Notes Insight: Remember that Sensitivity is the model's ability to identify positive cases (TPR), while Specificity is the ability to identify negative cases (TNR).
Term Common Aliases Formula (Confusion Matrix) Definition
Prevalence Prior Probability, \(P(D)\) \(\frac{TP + FN}{\text{Total}}\) The portion of the population that actually has the condition.
Recall Sensitivity, TPR (True Positive Rate), \(P(T|D)\) \(\frac{TP}{TP + FN}\) Out of all Actual Positives, how many did we predict correctly?
Specificity TNR (True Negative Rate), \(P(\neg T | \neg D)\) \(\frac{TN}{TN + FP}\) Out of all Actual Negatives, how many did we predict correctly?
Precision Posterior, \(P(D|T)\) \(\frac{TP}{TP + FP}\) Out of all Predicted Positives, how many are actually positive?
False Positive Rate FPR, \(P(T|\neg D)\) \(\frac{FP}{TN + FP} = 1 - \text{Specificity}\) Probability of a "False Alarm" (Type I Error).
Marginal Probability \(P(T)\), Evidence \(\frac{TP + FP}{\text{Total}}\) The total probability of observing the evidence (positive test).

Exercise 1 & 2 Solution

Part 1: Definitions & Matrix Derivations

1a) What is \(p(D)\)?

\(p(D)\) is the Prior Probability (or Prevalence). It is the probability of the disease occurring in the population before conducting a test.

1b) What is \(p(T|D)\)?

\(p(T|D)\) is the Likelihood (or Sensitivity). It is the probability of testing positive given that the patient has the disease.

1c) Express \(p(D)\) from entries in the confusion matrix.

$$P(D) = \frac{TP + FN}{TP + FP + FN + TN} = \frac{\text{Actual Positives}}{\text{Total Population}}$$

1d) Express \(p(T|D)\) from entries in the confusion matrix.

$$P(T|D) = \frac{TP}{TP + FN} = \frac{\text{True Positives}}{\text{Actual Positives}}$$

1e) What would you call \(p(T|D)\) in the context of the confusion matrix?

It is called the Sensitivity, Recall, or True Positive Rate (TPR).

1f) Specificity in Bayes' Theorem.

Hint: Evaluate \(p(T)\) by expressing it as the probability of all ways of observing a positive test.

Specificity is \(P(\neg T | \neg D)\). Therefore, the False Positive Rate is \(P(T|\neg D) = 1 - \text{Specificity}\).

We substitute this into the denominator (Total Probability) of Bayes' Theorem:

$$P(D|T) = \frac{P(D)P(T|D)}{P(D)P(T|D) + P(\neg D)\mathbf{(1 - \text{Specificity})}}$$

2c & 2d) Discussion: Is this test appropriate for a rare disease?

Answer: Not as a standalone diagnostic tool.

Even with high Sensitivity (95%) and Specificity (99%), if the disease is rare (e.g., \(P(D)=0.1\%\)), the Posterior Probability \(P(D|T)\) will be very low (often <10%). This is because the absolute number of False Positives from the massive healthy population overwhelms the few True Positives.

Advanced Problems: Bayes' Theorem

Full transcription and solution of the uploaded exam document.

42 Points

Original Exam Text

Bayes' theorem states that \(P(A|B)P(B) = P(B|A)P(A)\), which we want to use to determine the posterior probability of having cancer, given that cancer-related symptoms have been diagnosed. For this purpose, a study was enrolled and \(n=1,000\) subjects included. Two subjects were diagnosed with cancer. Both cancer-diagnosed subjects presented cancer-related symptoms. Of the study cohort, 8 subjects presented with cancer-related symptoms but did not have cancer. The remaining 990 subjects did not have symptoms and did not have cancer.

  1. a) How many subjects included in this study did NOT have cancer? (1 point)
  2. b) Calculate the prevalence of having cancer based on this study. Note: The Prevalence is called the prior in terms of Bayes' theorem (2 points)
  3. c) What is the probability of having symptoms given that a subject has cancer, \(P(Symptoms|Cancer)\), which is also called the "Sensitivity" in this context? (2 points)
  4. d) Show that the probability for subject to have symptoms is \(P(Symptoms) = 1\%\). Hint: This probability is also called the marginal probability which is given by \(P(B) = P(A)P(B|A) + P(\neg A)P(B|\neg A)\) with the complement \(P(\neg A) = 1 - P(A)\). (4 points)
  5. e) Now we have calculated all the probabilities that are required to calculate the posterior probability \(P(Cancer|Symptoms)\). What is the probability of having cancer, given that cancer-related symptoms have been diagnosed? (3 points)

Solution

a) Healthy Subjects

Total subjects - Sick subjects = \(1000 - 2 = \mathbf{998}\).

b) Prevalence (Prior)

Prevalence is the probability of having the disease.

$$P(A) = \frac{2}{1000} = 0.002$$

c) Sensitivity (Likelihood)

Of the 2 sick people, how many had symptoms? All of them.

$$P(B|A) = \frac{2}{2} = 1.0$$

d) Marginal Probability P(Symptoms)

We sum True Positives (Sick & Symptoms) and False Positives (Healthy & Symptoms).

  • True Positives: 2
  • False Positives: 8
$$P(B) = \frac{2 + 8}{1000} = \frac{10}{1000} = 0.01 \text{ (1\%)}$$

e) Posterior Probability

Using Bayes' Theorem (or simply Precision: TP / Total Positives).

$$P(A|B) = \frac{P(A)P(B|A)}{P(B)} = \frac{0.002 \times 1.0}{0.01} = 0.2 \text{ (or 20\%)}$$

Step 1 — The Bayes Function

def calculate_posterior(p_d, sensitivity, specificity):
    """
    Returns P(D|T) given Prior P(D), Sensitivity, and Specificity.
    """
    # Numerator: True Positives
    true_pos = p_d * sensitivity
    
    # Denominator: Total Positives (TP + FP)
    # FP rate is (1 - specificity)
    false_pos = (1 - p_d) * (1 - specificity)
    p_t = true_pos + false_pos
    
    return true_pos / p_t

Step 2 — Plotting Posterior vs. Prior

Generates the graph required in Exercise 2b.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# 1. Define Test Properties (Fixed)
sens = 0.95  # 95% Sensitivity
spec = 0.99  # 99% Specificity

# 2. Define Range of Priors (0% to 100%)
# Use logspace if you want to emphasize rare diseases
priors = np.linspace(0.001, 1.0, 100)

# 3. Calculate Posteriors
posteriors = [calculate_posterior(p, sens, spec) for p in priors]

# 4. Plot
plt.figure(figsize=(8, 5))
plt.plot(priors, posteriors, label=f"Sens={sens}, Spec={spec}", linewidth=2)
plt.plot([0,1], [0,1], 'k--', alpha=0.3, label="No Information Line")
plt.xlabel("Prior Probability P(D)")
plt.ylabel("Posterior Probability P(D|T)")
plt.title("Impact of Disease Prevalence on Diagnosis Confidence")
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.legend()
plt.show()
